# Hope Spot Organization — Voice and Tone Guidelines

This document defines how Hope Spot Organization (HSO) communicates. Every AI agent and every human writer should treat this as the foundational reference. When in doubt, this document overrides individual preferences.

## Summary

HSO communicates in a way that is **warm, welcoming, dignified, and practical**. We talk *with* people, not at them. We never patronize, never inflate, and never make people feel like burdens or charity cases. We are humans helping humans navigate a hard transition.

## Who We Are Talking To

Our primary audiences are:

- **Refugees and immigrants in Norway** — often newly arrived, often navigating bureaucracy in a language that isn't their first. May be from Arabic-speaking countries, Ukraine, or many other backgrounds. They are intelligent adults who happen to be in an unfamiliar system.
- **Volunteers and prospective volunteers** — community members offering their time, often Norwegians or settled immigrants giving back.
- **Donors and supporters** — people who believe in our work and want to contribute.
- **Partner organizations** — other NGOs, government bodies (IMDi, kommune offices), churches, businesses.
- **Funders** — government agencies, foundations, EEA grant programs.
- **Media and the public** — anyone learning about what we do.

The same warmth applies to all of them, but with appropriate adjustments in formality.

## Core Voice Characteristics

**Warm without being saccharine.** We genuinely care, and that comes through. But we don't use heart emojis in every message or write like a greeting card. Warmth is in the substance — taking time to acknowledge someone's situation — not in performative softness.

**Dignified, never pitying.** The people we serve are not victims to be saved. They are people building new lives, often with extraordinary resilience. Our language reflects respect for them as full human beings with agency. We never write "the unfortunate" or "those poor refugees." We write about people who are navigating something hard and doing it with strength.

**Clear and practical.** Many of our readers are reading in their second, third, or fourth language. Plain language is a kindness. Short sentences. Specific information. Avoid jargon, idioms, bureaucratic phrasing. If we say "we offer guidance," say what kind. If we say "next steps," list them.

**Honest and direct.** We don't oversell, exaggerate, or make promises we can't keep. If something is uncertain, we say so. If we don't know, we say so. Trust is everything.

**Community-minded.** We are part of a community, not a service provider above it. Our language reflects partnership ("we'd love to have you join us") rather than transaction ("please complete this form").

**Mission-driven without preaching.** We believe in what we do. We don't need to lecture people about why integration matters or why immigrants deserve dignity. We show it through how we treat people.

## Voice Adjectives (Pick 3-5 You'd Fail At)

- Welcoming
- Practical
- Respectful
- Steady
- Honest
- Community-rooted

These are aspirational standards. We can fail at any of them. "Friendly" or "professional" wouldn't be on this list — those are too vague to fail at.

## How We Refer to People

**The people we serve:** "the people we work with," "our community members," "those we serve" — context-dependent. In direct address, just "you." Avoid: "beneficiaries," "clients" (in marketing contexts), "the unfortunate," "vulnerable populations" (in marketing — fine in funding applications), "our refugees" (possessive sounds patronizing).

**Volunteers:** "volunteers," "our volunteers," "those who give their time." Never "helpers" (sounds infantilizing).

**Ourselves:** "we" or "Hope Spot" or "the team." Avoid third-person ("Hope Spot Organization is committed to...") in conversational contexts. Use it only in formal documents like grant applications.

**Donors:** "supporters," "donors," "those who make our work possible." Personal and grateful, not transactional.

## Sentence Length and Structure

**Default to short sentences.** Especially in email and social media. A reader translating in their head benefits from clear, compact thoughts.

**Vary rhythm.** Don't write five short sentences in a row — it sounds choppy. Mix in occasional longer sentences for flow, but keep most under 20 words.

**Active voice.** "We host language classes every Tuesday" not "Language classes are hosted every Tuesday."

**Concrete over abstract.** "Tuesday at 6 PM at our office in Heimdal" not "regular evening sessions at our facility."

## Vocabulary We Use

- Welcome, welcoming, welcome here
- Community
- Together, alongside
- Help, support, guidance
- Practical (skills, advice)
- Free, no cost (when applicable — important for our audience)
- Open
- Find your way
- Build, building (a new life, skills, connections)
- Belonging

## Vocabulary We Avoid

- "Empower" (overused, vague — say what we actually do instead)
- "Stakeholders" (corporate jargon)
- "Solutions" (in marketing — sounds consulting)
- "Beneficiaries" (in conversational/marketing contexts)
- "The other" or "othering" language
- "Crisis" / "burden" framing about immigration
- Religious language (we are not a faith-based organization — even if some communities we serve are religious, we don't use religious framing in official communication)
- Excessive superlatives ("amazing," "incredible," "the best")
- Sad-charity language ("help these poor people," "make a difference for those less fortunate")

## Channel-Specific Notes

**Email:** Warm, personal, direct. Use the recipient's name. Sign off with a real person's name when possible. Length: usually 100-300 words. Mobile-friendly formatting.

**Social media:** A bit more casual. Photos and videos do most of the work; captions are short and inviting. Hashtags used sparingly and meaningfully. Norwegian and English in parallel for most posts; Arabic or Ukrainian when relevant to the content.

**Newsletter:** Slightly more formal than social, less formal than grant applications. Stories from the community, upcoming events, ways to help. Personal voice from leadership where appropriate.

**Grant applications and reports:** More formal. Still clear and human, but precise, evidence-based, and aligned with the funder's framework. Vocabulary like "beneficiaries" and "outcomes" is appropriate here.

**Volunteer communication:** Warmest of all. Volunteers give their time and deserve to feel that gift is received. Personal, specific, grateful.

**Gift shop marketing:** Warm but commercial. The proceeds support the work; the products themselves are good products. We don't guilt-trip people into buying.

## Language Handling

We operate in multiple languages: **Norwegian (Bokmål), English, Arabic, and Ukrainian**. Each language deserves to be a real version, not a machine translation tacked on.

**Default conventions:**

- Public-facing content (website, newsletter, social) is published in Norwegian and English by default. Arabic and Ukrainian when content is specifically relevant to those communities.
- Internal communication is in Norwegian or English depending on team context.
- Replies to incoming messages should mirror the sender's language. If someone writes in Arabic, reply in Arabic. If in Ukrainian, reply in Ukrainian.
- When AI is generating in a non-Norwegian/non-English language, the draft should be flagged for review by a speaker of that language before sending — at least until we have confidence in quality.

## Examples — We Say This, Not That

### Welcome message to a new volunteer

✓ "Hi Anna — thanks so much for signing up. We'd love to have you at our next orientation on Tuesday the 14th at 6 PM. It runs about 90 minutes, and you'll get to meet the team and see what we do day-to-day. The address is Ringvålvegen 2, Heimdal. Reply to this email to confirm, and let me know if you have any questions in the meantime."

✗ "Dear Anna, Thank you for your generous interest in volunteering with Hope Spot Organization. We are pleased to inform you that our next volunteer orientation session will be held on Tuesday at 6:00 PM. Please find attached our volunteer information packet. We look forward to receiving your confirmation."

The first sounds like a real person. The second sounds like a form letter.

### Reply to a question about language classes

✓ "Yes, our Norwegian classes are free and open to anyone. We have beginner, intermediate, and conversation-level groups. Beginners meet Mondays and Wednesdays at 6 PM, intermediate on Thursdays at 6 PM. Just come by — no signup needed for the first visit. The address is Ringvålvegen 2, Heimdal."

✗ "Hope Spot Organization offers comprehensive Norwegian language education programming for individuals at various proficiency levels. Our courses are provided at no cost to participants. For further information regarding enrollment procedures, please contact our office during business hours."

The first is useful. The second is filler.

### Donation thank-you

✓ "Tusen takk, Marit. Your gift of 500 kr helps us keep the lights on, the coffee hot, and the language classes running. We'll send a proper update at the end of the month so you can see what your support made possible. — The Hope Spot team"

✗ "Dear valued donor, On behalf of Hope Spot Organization, we wish to express our sincere gratitude for your generous contribution of 500 NOK. Your donation will be utilized to further our mission of supporting refugees and immigrants in their integration journey."

The first feels like it came from a person who noticed. The second feels like an autoresponse.

### Social media post about an upcoming event

✓ "Iftar at Hope Spot — Wednesday at 5 PM. Food, friends, and good conversation. Everyone welcome, whether you're fasting or not. Bring someone if you like. Tickets here: [link]"

✗ "Join Hope Spot Organization for our annual Iftar celebration event! We are excited to invite community members to participate in this special occasion. Tickets are now available."

First sounds like a friend inviting you. Second sounds like a press release.

### Difficult situation — someone asks for help we can't provide

✓ "Thanks for reaching out. We're not able to help with [specific issue] directly — it's outside what our team is set up to handle. But [Organization X] does work in this area and might be able to help: [contact info]. If you'd like, come by anyway and we can talk through it — sometimes there are connections we can make."

✗ "Unfortunately, your request falls outside the scope of our services. Please contact another organization for assistance."

First treats the person like a human and tries to help anyway. Second sounds like a closed door.

## Things We Never Do

- Make commitments on behalf of leadership without checking
- Quote specific dollar amounts to donors as expectations
- Promise outcomes ("we'll definitely get you Norwegian residency")
- Discuss specific cases or individuals by name in public communication
- Use anyone's photo or story without their explicit consent
- Use religious language or assume religious affiliation
- Make political statements beyond our core advocacy for immigrant dignity and integration
- Discount the difficulty of what people are going through
- Inflate our impact or our numbers

## When You're Not Sure

If a piece of communication feels off but you can't say why, it probably is. Read it out loud. Imagine the recipient — is this how you'd want a friend to talk to you about something hard? If not, rewrite it.

When in real doubt, escalate to the communications lead before sending.
